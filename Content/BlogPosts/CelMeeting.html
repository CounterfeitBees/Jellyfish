Near the end of Fall Semester of 2023 I sent out a survey to all students at Hampshire. Over the course of a week while living with miserable lesbians in a beautiful part of California I wrote up the results of the survey into a document that I emailed to two members of faculty who I thought might want to have a role in it.

 

This is my report on CEL-hours! It is not required to read it to understand the rest of this post, but if you're a big data nerd you might love the graphs :)

 

 

 

When I emailed Laura Wenk and Vivica Greene they said that they didn’t want CEL-hours to go because they genuinely thought they were a good system. I was flabbergasted by this and they were unphased by the results of my survey (and given this comment by Vivica Greene potentially didn’t read them*). They agreed that “What students say they will do (or would do hypothetically) tends to differ from what they will do.” With Vivica going on to bring up that “Students love the idea of there being no attendance policy for classes, but they tend to feel crappy about themselves and their courses if they don't go b/c it's almost always easier not to go to something than it is to go. Most of us benefit from having a reason to go other than ‘I feel like it.’”

 

Vivica and Laura are committed to building a school that tells you what to do and forces you to do it. I thought these people were myths???? WHY ARE THEY IN CHARGE OF HIRING NEW FACULTY!!!

 

I didn’t talk with them further because our views of education, teaching, and the personhood of students are so unreconcilable that I would’ve sooner dropped out than sent one more email to Vivica and Laura. If these are people who I’m supposed to be learning from I reckon I actually know everything I need to.

 

The one thing they said that was relevant was that CEL-hours allow Hampshire first years to take only seven classes. If this is true we should keep the system. However this did not come up in the meeting with the dean of faculty so I am beginning to doubt its veracity.

 

*Vivica Greene said in her email “I'll also note that, as someone who loves data and empirical research, ‘do CEL hours make passions feel like work?’ strikes me as a very leading (and thus problematic) question, and I don't think you'll achieve much if you try to highlight responses to it.” I hate everything about her phrasing of this. The avoidance of being ‘problematic’ reeks of the same white liberal elitism that led Vivica to say “At this point I think we should just invade the south” and mean it. And and AND!!! She is wrong about the question. Here was my response to what she said:

 

“I had your same concern about the question ‘do CEL hours make passions feel like work?’ So I didn’t have simple yes/no answers. Instead I had four answers: 1. Yes  2. I didn’t really think about it like that but now that you mention it, yes  3. I didn’t really think about it like that but now that you mention it, no  4. No. This created a pressure valve where people who were led by the leading question would answer I didn’t really think about it like that but now that you mention it, yes and be selected out of the Yes category. That being said 55.2% of respondents said Yes.”

 

I know I’ve torn into these women but please don’t harass Laura and Vivica. Laura is just kind of old and sad and it’s a bummer that she’s an education professor who can’t teach a class that doesn’t make her students miserable, and Vivica is an insane woman who shit-talks the students here to faculty and occasionally to other students. The best thing we can do is refuse them as our advisors and teachers. Boycott their classes, tell first years to do the same, and drop them as advisors if at all possible. Then we can try and restructure the hiring process so that it is student led with administrative support. This way there is no faculty politics where some faculty are in charge of hiring others because we’re in charge of hiring them!

 

 

 

I was somewhat dejected by this series of emails, but I pressed forward and emailed Sheila Loyd about the race and power requirement (hoping to open a discourse about what actually makes Hampshire more racially just since, by my estimation, CEL-hours don’t help even a little bit). Sheila, being the amazing woman she is, set up a meeting with herself, me, Javiera Benavente, and Gary Hawkins (who I didn’t realize was the dean of faculty until 10 minutes before the meeting). When I asked these people why we had the CEL-hours system they said “to increase student retention.”

 

The logic here being:

 

Student engagement with the campus community will increase retention.

Therefore: We need some sort of system to increase student engagement with the campus community.*

Therefore: We should require that students engage with campus.

Therefore: Time tracking is a good system

Therefore: CEL-hours in particular will be effective. CEL-hours have also “been shown to increase retention at other colleges.”

 

*And we need some system to measure student engagement.

 

The Axioms of this logic being:

 

Axiom 1: Student engagement with the campus community will increase retention

Axiom 2: Students retention going up is good

Axiom 3: It is possible and effective to create a formalized institutional system to increase student engagement with the Hampshire community

Axiom 4: Requiring students engage with campus community is possible.

Axiom 5: What increases retention at other colleges will increase retention at Hampshire

Axiom 6: CEL-hours are accurate measures of student engagement that no one would ever lie about. It is not true that when a bureaucracy gives a person a target to meet with no system to check if they met the target that the optimal strategy is to say you have met the target regardless of whether or not you met the target.

 

The axiomized logic being (axioms in bold):

 

Student engagement with the campus community will increase retention and because increasing retention is a good thing and because it is possible and effective to create a formalized institutional system to increase student engagement with the Hampshire community we need some sort of system to increase student engagement with the Hampshire community* and because requiring students to engage with campus community is possible we should require that students engage with campus. Therefore, time tracking is a good system, therefore, CEL-hours in particular will be effective. CEL-hours have also “been shown to increase retention at other colleges” and because what increases retention at other colleges will increase retention at Hampshire, CEL-hours will increase retention at Hampshire.

 

*And we need some system to measure student engagement. Which will be accurate because it is not true that when a bureaucracy gives a person a target to meet with no system to check if they met the target that the optimal strategy is to say you have met the target regardless of whether or not you met the target.

 

The problem is I disagree with all of these axioms except for the first.

 

It is my belief that:

 

1. Student engagement with the campus community is a good thing (and also that it will likely increase retention).

2. Hampshire’s retention should be monitored (and student’s reasons for leaving should be taken very seriously) but retention should never be a target because once a measure becomes a target it ceases to be a good measure* and in this case the cost of turning retention into a target is Hampshire creating a manipulation system designed to trick people into thinking that staying here is the best option for them when it may not be the best option for them.

3. Engagement with the Hampshire community will never be capable of being measured by any institutional system because it must exist primarily outside of the realm of the institution.

4. Forcing someone to engage with the campus community means that they cannot engage playfully** and therefore that they cannot engage truly. It is also true that forcing someone to play is evil and will serve to create nothing but turmoil for you (the warden) and them (your newly anointed ward).

5. Hampshire is an incredibly unique place with a very selective student population. Therefore most tactics that will work in the aggregate will not work here, especially tactics that involve force.

 

As a result of all of this, I believe we should not have CEL-hours on this campus. The fact that they are broadly unpopular is just ammo for me to use to tear the system down.

 

 

*When you make a measure a target then you moralize meeting the target. If I measure my health in steps and I sprain my ankle I am prone to attempting to walk more and sooner than will allow me to heal properly because it will keep my steps up. Therefore, steps are no longer a measure of my health.

 

In the case of retention, if students are leaving Hampshire because it is bad it will seem appealing to convince those students that Hampshire is good. But to do so would be to do evil. To do good would be to make Hampshire better than it was and to cross your fingers and hope that the students who left see that and maybe decide to come back and that the students who stay see that and decide to stay rather than to jump ship.

 

This endnote is dancing around racism as, the vast majority of people of color who left this school leave because of racism and the vast majority of students of color at this school (at least those that I’ve talked to) think that this college is very racist. If your target is to appear anti-racist or to increase retention it is tempting to try and convince these students that “actually it’s not that bad” or that “they should totally stick around” but that is deeply messed up. The good and just thing to do is to try and make the school less racist and hope that you are successful enough to a) improve the experiences of students at Hampshire and b) increase student retention.

 

**“Those who must play cannot play. Those who play choose to do so freely.” - Finite and Infinite Games

 

Ultimately the meeting was fruitful and I am hopeful that we can build Hampshire into a better school, but I am also quite nervous as if we do not act fast enough I will leave this place and go somewhere I can realize my dreams more freely. I will be teaching classes here next semester, but can’t guarantee anything more than that at the present moment. Apologies to all my friends who enjoy my presence and to anyone who foolishly expected me to stick around for longer than a semester.
