I believe I took the train to reach the little garden because it was on the edge of town. The town was Woodstock New York, but it was very much a California town and looked nothing like Woodstock.

There were protesters in Woodstock for an hour every day. I can't remember why, but they had some mighty purpose and I believed in their cause.

I was bitter about these protesters because it felt like they were making a lot of noise but weren't using that noise to do anything. I've been thinking a lot about social change. Mostly because I used to care so much to the point that it was debilitating. I saw climate catastrophe and said "oh no! We must do something!" and was met with a resounding "eh" from the people around me, except those who were too deppressed to think about the climate who would say "I can't hear you talk about this right now because it makes me tooo sad."

I kind of miss caring so much that it hurt. But I do not miss the fact that I was very ineffective when I was in that much pain.

On my hand I have calloses, or at least hardened skin. I do not know exactly when this started; the day where I suddenly had a calloused hand. But I do know when the process started. It was two years ago when I started playing guitar, and one year ago when I started climbing.

Since then I've thought a lot about calloses. About their nessicity, and about their drawbacks. Neither of my hands are as sensitive as they were nine seasons ago, but I would certaiunly cut them up more while climbing and be in significantly more pain while playing guitar if they were still that sensitive.

What calloses have I built up surrounding social change?

When I cared a lot about social change I cared so much that I barely cared about anything else. I cared so much that it didn't make sense that anyone could care about anything else. I was blinded to the idea that anything else existed and caught in my own way of seeing the world.

Now I see how much there is to love and care about. And now I see how foolish it is to pick one thing and declare it the only thing worthy of love and care. But I am still conflicted about the morality of looking at a man-made apocolypse and saying "I don't care right now" or even "I don't care at all."

It is true. I don't care. But that truth feels cold. That truth is cold. It stings.

I miss the fire. I miss a community that recognizes the problems of the world and stands up to them. I miss the feeling of belonging. Of having a place where me caring about climate change is a part of a whole, not my own little atomized world.

At some point in my teens I was lucky enough to be in two or three sturdy communities that made a place for me and whatever I wanted to put there. At some point in my teens they disapeered. Or I left. And I tried in vein to bring them back.

...

During World War II the United States of America flew supplies into small islands in the pacific ocean and used them as staging grounds to fight the Japonese Empire. On these islands there were people who had never seen planes or penicilin before. When the fighting was done and the Americans left some of the people on some of the islands built statues of planes out of sticks and wood. The story that anthropologists tell about these statues is that they are "trying to bring the planes back."

This is something we all do. Something good is now gone and we build the wrong part of it to bring it back. My communities were gone, so I tried to learn more, to understand better, the problems of the world and how to overcome them (for that was my role in those communities). The problem was, no matter how good my skills, I would never bring them back.
