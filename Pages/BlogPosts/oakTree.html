I had some strange dreams last night and I think they are beutiful! All that I remember was that I planted a cigerete tree in an abandoned garden bed. All who witnessed laughed merrily becuase I do not know how to garden, let alone plant a cigerete tree.

Upon reflection I believe I took the train to reach the little garden because it was on the edge of town. The town was Woodstock New York, but it was very much a californian town and looked nothing like Woodstock.

There were protesters in Woodstock for an hour every day. I can't remember why, but they had some mighty purpose and I believed in their cause. I disagreed with their tactic, but only becuase I had an inkling of a next step; an actual traget not mearly an orginizing of bodies.

I was bitter about this at first but after some time sitting on the train I realized that the only people who will go through with interupting the business of the target I had in mind were the people protesting every day in downtown Woodstock. Not all of them of course, but enough to get done what I desired.

































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































































I've been thinking a lot about social change. Mostly because I used to care so much to the point that it was debilitating. I saw climate catastrophe and said "oh no! We must do something!" and was met with a resounding "eh" from the people around me, except those who were too deppressed to think about the climate who said "I can't hear you talk about this right now."

I miss caring so much that it hurt so much when things went wrong. But I do not miss the fact that I was very ineffective when I was in that much pain.

On my hand I have calloses, or at least hardened skin. I do not know exactly when this started; the day where I suddenly had a calloused hand. But I do know when the process started. It was nine seasons ago, and also five. When I started playing guitar, and when I started climbing.

Since then I've thought a lot about calloses. About their nessicity, and about their drawbacks. Neither of my hands are as sensitive as they were nine seasons ago, but I would certaiunly cut them up more climbing and be in significantly more pain while playing guitar if they were that sensitive still.

What calloses have I built up surrounding social change?

When I cared a lot about social change I cared so much that I barely cared about anything else. I cared so much that it didn't make sense that anyone could care about anything else. I was blinded to the idea that anything else existed-argueably more calloused than I am now-and caught in my own way of seeing the world.

. . .

...

Now I see how much there is to love and care about. And now I see how foolish it is to pick one thing and declare it the only thing worthy of love and care. But I am still conflicted about the morality of looking at a man-made apocolypse and saying "I don't care right now" or even "I don't care at all."

It is true. I don't care. But that truth feels cold. That truth is cold. It stings.

...

. . .

I miss the fire. I miss a community that recognizes these* as the problems of the world and stands up to them. I miss the feeling of belonging. Of having a place where me caring about climate change is a part of a whole, not my own little atomized world.

At some point in my teens I was lucky enough to be in two or three sturdy communities that made a place for me and whatever I wanted to put there. At some point in my teens they disapeered. Or I left. Or we were seperated. And I tried in vein to bring them back.

During World War II the United States of America flew supplies into small islands in the pacific ocean and used them as staging grounds to fight the Japonese Empire. On these islands there were people who had never seen planes or penicilin before. When the fighting was done and the Americans left some of the people on some of the islands built statues of planes out of sticks and wood. The story that anthropologists tell about these statues is that they are "trying to bring the planes back."

This is something we all do. Something good is now gone and we build the wrong part of it to bring it back. My communities were gone, so I tried to learn more, to understand better, the problems of the world and how to overcome them (for that was my role in those communities). The problem was, no matter how good my skills, I would never bring them back.
